### Expert Q&A cards  ⚠️ _names are placeholders — see top of doc_
1. **Q — How do I swaddle — and what if she hates it?**
   - **A:** Lay the blanket as a diamond, fold the top corner, one arm down and wrap snug across, then the other — tight at the chest, loose at the hips. If she fights it, try arms-up or a zip sleep sack; some babies just sleep better unswaddled, and that's completely fine.

2. **Q — Why won't she sleep unless I'm holding her?**
   - **A:** Newborns are wired for closeness — your heartbeat and warmth literally regulate hers. Contact naps now don't create bad habits. Once feeding's established you can start setting her down drowsy-but-awake to practice; until then, hold away.
   - _attributed to:_ Renee Voss · pediatric sleep consultant · villie expert
3. **Q — Is it safe for her to sleep this much?**
   - **A:** Yes — 14–17 hours a day in short 2–4 hour stretches is exactly right. Until she's back to birth weight, wake her to feed if she goes past 4 hours; after that, let her lead.
   - _attributed to:_ Dr. Marcus Hill, MD · pediatrician · villie expert

### Expert Q&A cards  ⚠️ _names are placeholders — see top of doc_
1. **Q — Is she latched right — and getting enough?**
   - **A:** A good latch tugs but doesn't pinch — wide-open mouth, chin buried in the breast. And you measure 'enough' by output, not ounces: six or more wet diapers a day by the end of week one means she's getting plenty. Count diapers, not minutes.
   - _attributed to:_ Dana Reyes, IBCLC · lactation consultant · villie expert
2. **Q — My nipples are cracked — what do I do?**
   - **A:** Cracking almost always traces back to a shallow latch — get the mouth wider with more areola in. A little lanolin or your own expressed milk heals them between feeds. If it's bleeding or getting worse, see a lactation consultant — it's fixable.
   - _attributed to:_ Dana Reyes, IBCLC · lactation consultant · villie expert
3. **Q — How do I know it’s a growth spurt?**
   - **A:** Sudden round-the-clock feeding for a day or two — often around weeks 2–3 and again at 6 — is a growth spurt, not low supply. Feed on demand; your body catches up within about 48 hours.
   - _attributed to:_ Dr. Marcus Hill, MD · pediatrician · villie expert

### Expert Q&A cards  ⚠️ _names are placeholders — see top of doc_

### Expert Q&A cards  ⚠️ _names are placeholders — see top of doc_
1. **Q — How do I bathe her — and care for the cord?**
   - **A:** Until the cord drops off (usually 1–3 weeks), stick to sponge baths: warm room, damp cloth, wash the face and folds, pat dry. Keep the stump dry and open to air, fold the diaper below it, and let it fall off on its own. Redness, swelling, or a foul smell means call us.
   - _attributed to:_ Dr. Marcus Hill, MD · pediatrician · villie expert
2. **Q — What counts as a fever I should call about?**
   - **A:** Under 3 months, a rectal temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher is an automatic call — any hour, day or night. And don't give a newborn fever medicine before you talk to us.
   - _attributed to:_ Dr. Marcus Hill, MD · pediatrician · villie expert
3. **Q — Her skin is peeling and blotchy — normal?**
   - **A:** Both are normal in the early weeks — that outer layer sheds, and come-and-go red splotches (newborn rash) settle on their own. Keep everything fragrance-free. Call if there's pus, blistering, or it spreads fast.
   - _attributed to:_ Dr. Lena Ortiz · pediatric dermatologist · villie expert
